Fishing in the 'Loo

Today it plays host to some of the more opulent hotels and restaurants in town, but the area around Woolloomooloo's Finger Wharf has a tough, working class history. Today the streets of 'the 'Loo' remain predominantly low-income residential.

Before the arrival of Europeans, Woolloomooloo was 'Wulla Mulla', a site for corroborees as well as an excellent bay for fishing. It remained a fishing hub right up to the 1970s - today's Finger Wharf was once the site of Woolloomooloo's Fishmarkets, the centre of the New South Wales fishing industry before the Pyrmont Fish markets superseded it in the early 1900s.

Sicilians, Calabrians and Lipari Islanders made up a significant proportion of the fishing community in Woolloomooloo. Their fishing boats were tied in deepwater moorings at Brown's wharf, next to the Finger Wharf. In bad weather they could be seen mending their nets or playing cards at the wharf.

Most days the fishermen would string their nets out to dry, a common sight around the streets of Woolloomooloo. The fishing industry began to move away from Woolloomooloo when crucial areas were demolished for the construction of the eastern suburbs railway.
