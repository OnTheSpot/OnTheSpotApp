Saving Woolloomooloo

In the early 1970s the streets of Woolloomooloo were earmarked for redevelopment into high rise apartments and office towers which would have seen some additional 80,000 new workers and residents into the area. Then NSW Premier Robert Askin liked the plans so much he'd personally organised a $37.5m line of foreign credit for the development!

Local residents fought hard against the proposal, and in 1972 were joined in opposition to the plans by the Builders Labourers' Federation (BLF) who enforced a 'green ban' on the area, in turn attracting the support of the newly-elected Federal Labor Government, who had made an election pledge to intervene in the redevelopment scheme.When Gough Whitlam's Labor Government was elected into power they commissioned a new planning proposal for the area, which helped to break a gridlock between the City of Sydney and the NSW Government over their separate plans for the area.The resulting retention of Woolloomooloo's low income-income residential mix has since been hailed as a triumph of community-led urban environmental activism, and is studied by urban planners around the world.
