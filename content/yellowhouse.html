The Yellow House

The building at 59 Macleay St has art written all over it. Its yellow colour was inspired by Van Gogh's dream of an artists' refuge south of Paris, realised here by pop artist Martin Sharp in 1970 when he turned this establishment into Sydney's 'Yellow House'.The short time that Sharp and his cohort occupied this building - during 1970 and 1971 - are now regarded as the halcyon days of experimental pop art in Australia. Prior to their occupation the building had been a haven for many of Australia's best-known artists, welcomed by building owner Frank Clune, a Sydney writer who supported a number of Sydney's emerging abstract and modernist artists from the 1950s such as John Olsen, Robert Hughes, Robert Klippel, Stan Rapotec and John Drysdale.But when Martin Sharp returned from London the building's gallery space was no longer in use. Sharp - who'd been wowing global audiences with his dazzling psychedelic poster art for the Oz Magazine - found Sydney's art scene a bit dismal, and so decided to establish a surrealist-inspired conceptual art space here.The Yellow House became Australia's first multimedia performance art space, a 'happening' 24 hours a day. In line with its anti-establishment ethos, the works produced here were not constrained by disciplinary conformity - open to practitioners and performers practicing fine art, theatre, music and writing - at the time unique in the history of Australian art.
